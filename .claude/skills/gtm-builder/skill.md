# GTM Builder

Scale-stage go-to-market function builder. Takes a product with validated PMF and builds the commercial infrastructure to grow it beyond founder-led organic traction.

## The Failure Mode This Prevents

Organic growth has a ceiling, and most Scale-stage founders hit it before they've ever had to build a real GTM function. Signs: flattening user curves, rising customer acquisition costs, pipeline that only moves when the founder is personally involved. Founder hustle got you here, but scaling requires creating and implementing an actual go-to-market strategy. AI can help you build, then run, that complete GTM engine — but you have to actually design it first.

## Inputs Required

Before running GTM Builder, have:
1. Validated problem hypothesis (from `problem-hypothesis`)
2. Current ICP definition (from customer discovery data)
3. Core value proposition (what the product does and for whom)
4. Current acquisition channels and their unit economics (CAC, LTV, payback period)
5. Current customer base — size, segments, use cases
6. Top 3 competitors and their positioning
7. Pricing model

## Phase 1 — Market Segmentation

Most founders have a vague ICP. GTM requires a precise one — not because precision is philosophically better, but because every piece of GTM collateral requires a specific audience to be effective. "SMBs in fintech" can't be converted; "finance managers at Series A–C SaaS startups managing 5–50-person teams without a dedicated FP&A hire" can.

**Segmentation dimensions:**

*Firmographic:*
- Industry (primary + adjacent)
- Company stage / headcount / revenue
- Business model (B2B/B2C/marketplace)
- Geography (if relevant to the product)

*Technographic:*
- Stack signals that correlate with ICP fit (what tools they use)
- Maturity signals (DIY vs. buying solutions)

*Behavioral:*
- How they currently solve the problem (proxy for urgency and switching cost)
- Where they search for solutions
- What content they consume

**Segment prioritization matrix:**

| Segment | Size | Problem Urgency | CAC (est.) | LTV (est.) | Strategic Value |
|---------|------|-----------------|------------|------------|-----------------|
| Primary | | | | | |
| Secondary | | | | | |
| Future | | | | | |

Rank by (problem urgency × LTV) / CAC. Primary segment is where all GTM energy goes first.

**Buyer landscape mapping:**

For the primary segment:
- Who holds budget? (decision maker)
- Who influences the decision? (champion, influencer, blocker)
- Who uses the product? (end user — may not be the buyer)
- What does each care about? (buyer: ROI/risk/compliance; champion: career/recognition; user: speed/quality-of-life)

Different messages for different roles. A champion-facing message that leads with "saves you 2 hours a week" and a buyer-facing message that leads with "reduces reconciliation errors by 40%" are about the same product.

## Phase 2 — Messaging Architecture

**Core message hierarchy:**

*Level 1 — Category:* What game are you playing? Define the category you're in, or the new category you're creating.
> "The [category] for [specific person]"

*Level 2 — Value proposition:* What specifically do you do for them?
> "[Outcome] without [cost/tradeoff]"

*Level 3 — Proof points:* What makes that credible?
> Three specific, falsifiable claims. Metrics > testimonials > features.

*Level 4 — Differentiation:* Why you vs. alternatives?
> The one thing you do that competitors fundamentally can't (not won't — can't).

**Message-by-audience matrix:**

| Audience | Lead With | Proof Points | CTA |
|---------|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Buyer/Executive | Business outcome, ROI, risk | Metrics, case studies, compliance | Book a call |
| Champion/Manager | Career impact, team efficiency | Time saved, errors reduced | Start a trial |
| End User | Daily workflow improvement | Speed, quality, ease | Sign up |
| Analyst | Market positioning, differentiation | TAM, growth, technical moat | Analyst brief |
| Investor | Category, moat, unit economics | Revenue metrics, retention, NRR | Investor deck |

**Messaging test:**
For each message, ask:
- Does it speak to what the audience actually cares about (not what you wish they cared about)?
- Is it falsifiable? Could a skeptic challenge it? Good — make it defensible.
- Does it create urgency or just awareness?

## Phase 3 — Sales Playbook

**Playbook structure (minimum viable version):**

*Ideal Customer Profile Card:*
One page. Title, company profile, key identifiers, where to find them, typical objections, champion vs. buyer dynamics, deal timeline typical, average contract value range.

*Qualification criteria:*
Define MEDDIC or equivalent for your deal type:
- Metrics: What does "success" look like in numbers for this buyer?
- Economic buyer: Who approves the spend?
- Decision criteria: What does the buyer use to evaluate options?
- Decision process: What are the steps from "interested" to "signed"?
- Identify pain: Is the pain urgent, specific, and funded?
- Champion: Who inside the account is advocating for your product?

*Objection handling:*
For your top 5 objections, define: the underlying concern, the honest answer, the proof point.

| Objection | Real Concern | Response | Proof |
|-----------|-------------|---------|-------|
| "We built something internally" | Switching cost / NIH | | |
| "We're too small for this" | Budget / fit | | |
| "We need to see more customers like us" | Risk | | |
| "Let's revisit next quarter" | Not urgent enough | | |
| "How do we know you'll be around?" | Startup risk | | |

*Demo script:*
Define the demo flow as: problem statement → protagonist story → product walkthrough → specific outcome. The demo is a story, not a feature tour. The hero is the user, not the product.

*Follow-up cadence:*
Define the exact sequence of touchpoints from first meeting to signed contract. No "follow up when it makes sense" — define the triggers and templates.

## Phase 4 — Analyst and Investor Narrative

**Analyst briefing structure:**

Enterprise buyers will often check with analysts before signing multi-year contracts. Your analyst narrative needs to:
1. Define the category you're in (or creating)
2. Articulate the market problem and why now
3. Position against alternatives (direct, indirect, DIY)
4. Present your differentiation as structural, not feature-based
5. Provide proof of traction (metrics, customer names if permissioned)

**Investor metrics narrative:**

By Scale stage, you're talking to growth investors, not seed investors. The metrics they care about:
- ARR / MRR (and growth rate)
- Net Revenue Retention (NRR) — the most important single number
- CAC by channel
- LTV by segment
- Payback period
- Gross margin
- Rule of 40 (growth rate + profit margin ≥ 40%)

Build a one-page metrics narrative that shows: where you are, trajectory, and what the numbers reveal about your moat.

**Moat narrative (1 page):**

Answer the key question investors will ask: "If a well-funded incumbent copied your product today, would your users stay?"

Three sources of structural moat for an AI-native startup:
1. Accumulated user data and behavioral fingerprints (time-locked, can't be bought)
2. Domain expertise encoded in the product (industry-specific logic, edge cases, integrations)
3. Workflow lock-in (the deeper users build their workflows around your product, the higher the switching cost)

Write the moat narrative as: "Here's what we've built over [time period] that a competitor starting today couldn't replicate in under two years — and here's why."

## Phase 5 — GTM Execution Layer

Once strategy is defined, build the operational execution layer:

**Content pipeline:**
- Content types by audience (thought leadership for analysts, case studies for buyers, how-to for users)
- Publication cadence
- Distribution channels

**Outbound sequences:**
- ICP-specific outreach sequences (not generic)
- Follow-up logic
- Re-engagement triggers

**CRM hygiene:**
- Pipeline stages defined
- Stage-specific follow-up triggers
- Reporting cadence

**Metrics reporting:**
- Weekly GTM brief: pipeline, CAC by channel, conversion rates, content performance
- Monthly board narrative

## Rules

- Segment before messaging. Generic messaging is indistinguishable from noise. If the message works for everyone, it resonates with no one.
- The moat narrative must be specific. "We have a better product" is not a moat. "We have 18 months of behavioral data from 5,000 users in regulated industries that trained our edge-case logic" is a moat.
- Don't build the GTM execution layer until the strategy layer is locked. Automating outreach for the wrong message just means you burn your list faster.
- Revenue metrics are the only honest PMF evidence at Scale stage. Everything else is a leading indicator.
