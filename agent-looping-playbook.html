Agent operations · looping

Stop prompting every step. Build the loop.

Agent looping is the shift from giving an agent isolated tasks to designing a repeatable system that discovers, plans, executes, checks, and improves until the goal clears the requirements.

You are not driving every move anymore. You define the goal, constraints, eval gates, and stop condition. The agent or fleet runs the cycle.

The simple version

One agent researches, drafts, checks its draft, fixes the weak parts, and repeats until the work passes.

5core loop stages: discover → plan → execute → evaluate → iterate

The loop model

A loop is not a magic agent. It is a harness: a goal, a process, specialist roles, evaluation gates, and rules for when to continue or stop.

1. DiscoverResearch the market, customer, offer, competitors, or source material.
2. PlanBreak the goal into tasks, owners, constraints, and acceptance criteria.
3. ExecuteDraft, build, analyze, write, design, or produce the asset.
4. EvaluateScore the work against the goal, rubric, examples, and hard checks.
5. ImproveFix weak points, rerun the work, or hand back when the stop rule is hit.

One agent vs fleet looping

One-agent loop

Like a person redoing their own draft

Best for narrow work: rewrite this page, improve this email, research this buyer, tighten this offer, clean this deck.

Fleet loop

Like a team running a project

An orchestrator owns the goal. Specialists own steps. Subagents do narrow work. Eval gates keep the output from becoming slop.

Open looping vs closed looping

TypeWhat it isWhen to use itRisk
Open looping Exploratory. The agent has a goal and conditions, but broad room to try paths, discover angles, and build things you did not fully spec. New markets, new products, creative exploration, strategy discovery, research-heavy projects. High token cost. If the quality bar is loose, it becomes a slop machine.
Closed looping Bounded. A human designs the workflow, step order, evals, stop rule, and handoff points. Marketing production, content ops, CRO, email, ads, landing pages, repeatable client work. Less exploratory, but far more reliable and affordable today.
Practical rule: for most marketing work today, closed looping is the one that pays. Open looping is powerful, but expensive and easy to aim badly.

Examples we can use

Example 1

Landing page improvement loop

Goal: improve a landing page until it is clearer, more persuasive, and matches the buyer's intent.

  • Discover: analyze ICP, offer, competitors, objections, current page.
  • Plan: map hero, proof, mechanism, offer, CTA, FAQ.
  • Execute: rewrite sections.
  • Eval: score clarity, specificity, proof, objection handling, CTA strength.
  • Iterate: fix any section under 8/10, then produce final page.
Example 2

Ad creative loop

Goal: generate and refine ad concepts until we have testable creative angles.

  • Discover: pull customer language, reviews, competitor ads, pain points.
  • Plan: choose angles: pain, proof, contrarian, urgency, identity, demo.
  • Execute: create hooks, body copy, visuals, CTA.
  • Eval: reject generic claims, weak hooks, unclear offer, no thumb-stop.
  • Iterate: produce 3 stronger variants per winning angle.
Example 3

Email nurture loop

Goal: build a sequence that moves a lead from problem-aware to ready to book or buy.

  • Discover: segment stage, objections, proof, offer promise.
  • Plan: sequence jobs: educate, agitate, prove, compare, close.
  • Execute: draft each email.
  • Eval: check subject, first line, one job per email, CTA, continuity.
  • Iterate: rewrite weak emails and create subject-line tests.
Example 4

Content repurposing loop

Goal: turn one source into platform-native assets without flattening the voice.

  • Discover: extract thesis, claims, proof, examples, sharp lines.
  • Plan: choose formats: LinkedIn post, X thread, newsletter, short script.
  • Execute: draft each asset.
  • Eval: check voice fidelity, platform fit, specificity, usefulness.
  • Iterate: cut generic sections and add concrete examples.
Example 5

Offer research loop

Goal: discover stronger positioning and offer angles before building creative.

  • Discover: mine Reddit, reviews, sales calls, competitor pages, support tickets.
  • Plan: cluster pains, desires, alternatives, triggers, objections.
  • Execute: draft offer angles and promises.
  • Eval: score urgency, believability, differentiation, proof burden.
  • Iterate: refine top 3 angles and kill weak ones.
Example 6

SEO article loop

Goal: produce an article that satisfies search intent and is not generic AI filler.

  • Discover: SERP intent, competing pages, missing info, expert sources.
  • Plan: outline by searcher questions and decision path.
  • Execute: draft article with concrete examples.
  • Eval: check factuality, depth, skim value, originality, internal links.
  • Iterate: add missing evidence, remove fluff, improve headings.

Reusable closed-loop prompt

Use this as the operating brief for a bounded marketing loop.

Goal:
[Describe the business outcome, not just the asset.]

Inputs:
- Audience:
- Offer:
- Source material:
- Brand voice:
- Constraints:

Loop:
1. Discover the relevant context and extract useful facts.
2. Plan the work into clear steps.
3. Produce the first draft/output.
4. Evaluate it against the rubric below.
5. Revise anything below the pass threshold.
6. Repeat until every gate passes or you hit the stop rule.

Eval rubric:
- Clear target buyer: pass/fail
- Specific pain/desire: 1-10
- Strong proof: 1-10
- Differentiated angle: 1-10
- Concrete examples: 1-10
- No generic filler: pass/fail
- CTA or next step is obvious: pass/fail

Stop rule:
Stop when every scored item is 8/10 or higher and every pass/fail gate passes.
If the loop runs 3 times without improvement, stop and explain the blocker.

Fleet-loop template

Orchestrator

Owns the goal

Defines the outcome, splits the work, assigns specialists, watches constraints, and decides when to stop.

Specialists

Own the steps

Researcher, strategist, copywriter, designer, analyst, editor, verifier.

Eval gate

Prevents slop

Scores the output, rejects weak work, forces revision, and hands back when limits are hit.

Fleet example: landing page loop

Orchestrator: owns conversion goal and final decision.
Research agent: mines buyer language, competitors, objections, proof.
Strategy agent: chooses page angle and offer structure.
Copy agent: writes hero, sections, FAQ, CTA.
Critic agent: scores clarity, proof, specificity, differentiation.
Editor agent: revises low-scoring sections.
Verifier agent: confirms final output meets the rubric and flags remaining risk.

How to avoid slop

Do

Make the eval concrete

Use pass/fail gates, numeric thresholds, examples of good work, and hard stop rules.

Do

Limit the loop

Cap iterations, cap research time, define budget, and force handoff if quality stops improving.

Avoid

Loose goals

“Make it better” is too vague. Say what better means: conversion, clarity, proof, speed, signups, replies.

Avoid

Infinite exploration

Open loops are powerful, but without budget and standards they burn tokens and produce noise.

Bottom line: looping works when the system knows what good means. The eval gate is the difference between compounding quality and compounding slop.
